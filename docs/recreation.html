IMMIGRATION POLICIES & MIGRNANT MORTALITY

Experts and advocates worry current border control strategies are leading to migrant deaths, echoing problems of the past.

Humera Lodhi

Published October 2019

DATA AND GRAPHICS REPRODUCED FROM THE NEW YORK TIMES

In 1994, President Bill Clinton implemented a new United States Border Patrol plan, expanding the USBP's budget and manpower to larger than it had ever been before. The main element in this plan was known as "Prevention through Deterence." Thousands of people were attempting to cross from Mexico to America at cities along the border. This new strategy focused border control security and resources at these key cities seen as "easy entry points."

Much of the land along the Southern Bordern is sparsely populated desert-like terrain. The idea behind "Prevention Through Deterence" was to make the populated cities too gaurded to enter, leaving only the unihabited parts of the border unsecure. The original document states "with traditional entry and smuggling routes disrupted, illegal traffic will be deterred, or forced over more hostile terrain... illegal entrants crossing through remote, uninhabited expanses of land and sea along the border can find themselves in mortal danger.‚Äù

The goal was that open areas areas would difficult-- and dangerous-- to pass through, forcing people to stop trying to cross the border. Border crossings did decrease, but thousands still tried to get through, this time trekking over the "hostile terrain." As a results, migrant deaths increased exponentially. While no one could have predicted the death rate before implemention of the policy, it remained in place after the death toll became obvious.
